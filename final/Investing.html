It's important to understand that inflation significantly impacts your earnings over time. Suppose the example above, you contribute $500 monthly over 30 years, you'll end up with $290,000. But your real return on is closer to $180,000 once you adjust for 3% inflation. Historically, inflation has averaged around 3.1% over the past century, so to measure true growth, we have to consider the real value of money, not just the nominal increases.where Inflation rate is greater than the nominal interest rate, What seems like growth is actually a loss in real purchasing power. In an economists perspective, the borrow is actually the one who wins if the inflation is greater than the interest rate. Simply put - when you invest you are the loaner, whose gain is less than the depreciation of the currency in terms of real purchasing power.
